Research

My research interests sit at the boundary of self-supervised representation learning and scientific computer vision — building visual models that work well when data is plentiful but labels are not, and when the images come from microscopes, sensors, or simulations rather than from the web. I did not start there. The short version of how I got here runs through three projects, and you can open any of them in detail as you read.

The journey

My first real taste of research was at Shanghai Jiao Tong University, under Prof. Xiaodong Gu, working on a . The premise was simple and a little audacious: replace expensive finite-element simulations of heat flow inside high-temperature components with a neural network that predicts the full temperature field from geometry plus a handful of sensor readings. What I actually spent most of my time on was the data — turning messy STL meshes and sparse thermal readings into clean, voxelized tensors. That project taught me the lesson I now start every project with: the preprocessing layer is where most research silently fails, and fixing it is the highest-leverage thing you can do.

The temperature-field work made me want to understand the machinery underneath machine learning, not just the models on top of it. So at Michigan I went a layer down, into GPU programming, with a project for a LeNet-style CNN. I stacked four optimizations — constant memory, tiled shared memory, weight transposition, register-level accumulation — but the part that stuck with me was not any single trick. It was the discipline of letting Nsight Compute, not my intuition, decide what to optimize next. That habit of profiling before optimizing has become the way I think about systems generally, and it is why I am comfortable saying foundation-model research is increasingly a systems problem in disguise.

Those two threads — caring about the data pipeline, and caring about the hardware — both feed into what I am doing now. At Michigan I am , a foundation model for spatial proteomics, under Prof. Jie Liu. This is the work I most want to keep doing: self-supervised representation learning applied to biomedical imaging, where the data is abundant, the labels are scarce and expensive, and a good representation is worth far more than a clever loss. It pulls together everything the earlier projects taught me about data, models, and the systems that run them.

Where this is going

I am applying to Ph.D. programs in computer science with a focus on self-supervised representation learning, computer vision, and ML systems — especially in scientific and biomedical domains. The three projects above are public; my current KRONOS work is still in progress, and I am glad to discuss it directly with prospective advisors.

Writing

Tokenization inequity in LLMs. A research-essay project for TC-300 (Technical Communication) investigating the "tokenization tax" — the way modern BPE tokenizers use systematically more tokens to encode the same idea in Chinese, Arabic, or Hindi than in English, raising both the dollar cost and the latency of using large language models for speakers of those languages. The output is a blog post for a general technical audience, grounded in academic literature on tokenization, fairness, and inference economics. I am finishing the draft this semester.
