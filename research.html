Projects

My interests sit at the boundary of representation learning and scientific computer vision — building visual models that work well when data is plentiful but labels are not, and when the images come from microscopes, sensors, or simulations rather than from the web. Below are three projects that, taken together, cover the range of what I have worked on: applied scientific ML, low-level GPU optimization, and a team engineering project from earlier in my undergraduate. Each page is a short technical write-up of what I did and what I learned.

I am also currently doing further research in self-supervised representation learning at Michigan; that work is not yet public, but I am happy to discuss it directly with prospective advisors.

March – August 2025 · Shanghai Jiao Tong University

Voxel-Based 3D Temperature Field Reconstruction with a Spatiotemporal CNN

Under Prof. Xiaodong Gu, I built an end-to-end pipeline for predicting how temperature evolves inside high-temperature components. The work spanned data engineering (STL meshes and thermal sensor readings to voxelized tensors via Open3D) and model design (a 3D CNN for spatiotemporal prediction). The most useful lesson was that the preprocessing layer is where most projects silently fail.

3D CNN · Open3D · voxelization · scientific machine learning

Winter 2026 · EECS 471, University of Michigan

Optimizing a CUDA Convolution Kernel for a LeNet-style CNN

A from-scratch CUDA implementation of forward convolution for inference on Fashion-MNIST, then four progressively layered optimizations: constant-memory weights, tiled shared memory, weight transposition for coalesced reads, and register-level accumulation. Each step was profiled with Nsight Compute, so the design choices are anchored in measured occupancy and memory-throughput numbers rather than vibes.

CUDA · GPU programming · Nsight Compute · ML systems

Spring 2024 · UM-SJTU Joint Institute

Memory Chip — A 2D Game Built from Scratch in Six Weeks

A team project for Introduction to Engineering: a six-week sprint to build a breakout-style game in ELM on top of an unfamiliar custom 2D engine. I was programmer and visual designer, owning the UI layer and the scene-to-scene audio transitions. The project won Best Game Design at the course's final showcase. What I remember most from it is not the engineering — it is the lesson that the way out of a hard problem is to read the manual harder than feels reasonable.

ELM · game design · UI development · team engineering

Writing

Tokenization inequity in LLMs. A research-essay project for TC-300 (Technical Communication) investigating the "tokenization tax" — the way modern BPE tokenizers use systematically more tokens to encode the same idea in Chinese, Arabic, or Hindi than in English, raising both the dollar cost and the latency of using large language models for speakers of those languages. The output is a blog post for a general technical audience, grounded in academic literature on tokenization, fairness, and inference economics. I am finishing the draft this semester.
